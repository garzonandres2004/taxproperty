## His Live Property Evaluation Checklist

### Step 1: Initial Screen (2 Minutes)
**Quote:** *"There's thousands of properties sitting there right now that you can go buy for the back taxes owed mortgage free."*

**Quick Filters:**
- Opening bid vs estimated value (look for 50%+ below market)
- Property type (land vs house vs commercial)
- Location (urban vs rural)
- **Decision:** Skip obvious bad deals, deep dive on promising ones

### Step 2: Drive the Property
**Quote:** *"Everything that we're interested in bidding on, we're going to go ahead and drive that property."*

**What He's Looking For:**
- Physical condition from street
- Is there actually a structure there?
- Neighborhood quality
- Accessibility (can you get to it?)
- Occupancy signs (cars, lights, maintenance)

**Red Flags on Drive-By:**
- Major damage (fire, flood)
- Overgrown/abandoned appearance
- Dangerous neighborhood
- **Action:** Immediately eliminate from consideration

### Step 3: Check Property Value (ARV)
**Quote:** *"We're going to figure out how much it's worth... it's worth about 30 grand."*

**Tools Used:**
- Zillow Zestimate
- County assessed value
- Comparable sales

**Quick Math:**
- Opening bid: $2,500
- Estimated value: $30,000
- Potential quick sale: $24,999
- Gross profit: ~$22,499 (before costs)

**Quote:** *"If you got this for $2,500, you could probably put on the market for a quick sale, let's say like $24,999."*

### Step 4: Calculate Maximum Bid
**Quote:** *"Why would nobody buy this? Because it's worth about 30 grand."*

**His Analysis Process:**
1. Verify ARV (After Repair Value)
2. Estimate quick sale price (80% of ARV)
3. Subtract costs:
   - Municipal liens (if any)
   - Quiet title costs (~$3,500)
   - Desired profit margin
4. Result = Maximum Bid

**Quote on Municipal Liens:** *"If you do find a municipal lien, subtract that from your max bid."*

### Step 5: Verify Property Details
**Quote:** *"We always want to go by the property description here, not the location. We're always more interested in the parcel ID."*

**Critical Checks:**
- **Never trust the address alone**
- Always verify with parcel ID
- Cross-reference county GIS
- Check for recent changes

**Quote:** *"Never ever ever trust the property location."*

### Step 6: Sanity Check on Taxes
**Quote:** *"For one year of taxes, does it make sense on something that's worth close to a million dollars that there would only be $2,800 in taxes? No, that doesn't make sense, right?"*

**Red Flag:**
- Opening bid too low for property value
- Indicates hidden problems
- **Action:** Investigate why taxes are so low

**Green Light:**
- Tax amount proportional to value
- $28,000 on $962,000 property = 2.9% = makes sense

## His Yes vs NO Decision Framework

### IMMEDIATE YES (Green Lights)

1. **90% Below Market Value**
   - Quote: *"Properties sitting there right now that you can go buy for the back taxes owed mortgage free... sometimes up to 90% below market value"*
   - Opening bid is tiny fraction of value
   - Clear margin for profit

2. **Property Passes Drive-By**
   - Quote: *"This is a piece of land in Baytown, Texas. As you can see, nice piece of land there. Looks great."*
   - No major damage visible
   - Accessible and buildable

3. **Reasonable Tax History**
   - Taxes owed proportional to value
   - No red flags in opening bid
   - Recent delinquency (not decades old)

4. **Available OTC (Over the Counter)**
   - Quote: *"Sometimes over the counter can be good deal... first come, first serve"*
   - No auction competition
   - Time to do thorough research
   - Previously passed at auction (why?)

5. **Clear Parcel ID Match**
   - Address matches county records
   - Property lines verified with Regrid
   - No boundary disputes

### IMMEDIATE NO (Deal Killers)

1. **Landlocked or Inaccessible**
   - Quote: *"Make sure you're not buying some hunk of junk that is landlocked"*
   - No legal road access
   - Can't physically reach the property

2. **Tax Amount Doesn't Make Sense**
   - $2,800 taxes on $1M property
   - Indicates special circumstances
   - Potential hidden problems

3. **Occupied Without Research**
   - Tenant or owner still there
   - Eviction costs not factored
   - Redemption risk

4. **Too Good To Be True**
   - Quote: *"Why would nobody buy this?"*
   - If it's a great deal, ask why
   - Dig deeper before bidding

5. **Municipal Liens (in TX)**
   - Survive tax deed sale
   - Additional costs not in opening bid
   - Can be thousands of dollars

## Max Bid Calculation Process

### Formula He Uses:

```
Max Bid = Quick Sale Price - Costs - Profit

Where:
- Quick Sale Price = ARV × 0.80 (sell at 80% of market for fast sale)
- Costs = Municipal Liens + Quiet Title (~$3,500) + Holding Costs
- Profit = Your desired profit margin
```

**Property Details:**

## OTC (Over The Counter) Strategy

### What Is OTC?
**Quote:** *"Over the counter deed means there's no auction... first come, first serve."*

- Properties that didn't sell at auction
- Available directly from county
- No bidding competition
- Can research at your own pace

### Why OTC Can Be Good:
1. **No Competition** - Quote: *"No auction and that can be a great time"*
2. **Time to Research** - Not rushed by auction date
3. **Lower Prices** - Sometimes desperate to sell
4. **First Come First Serve** - Speed beats deep pockets

### Why OTC Can Be Bad:
1. **Passed for a Reason** - Quote: *"Why would nobody buy this?"*
2. **May Have Issues** - Hidden liens, access problems
3. **Still Need Full DD** - Don't skip research because it's OTC

### What Are They?
**Quote:** *"In Georgia what they have for over the counter is called a judicial in rem sale."*

- Georgia's version of OTC
